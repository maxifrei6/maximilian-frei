abstract: "This project compares microbial co-occurrence networks from the Atlantic and Pacific ocean basins using the GRUMP database. methodology: "GRUMP sample-by-ASV data were filtered by basin (upper 200 m), and association networks were estimated with SPIEC-EASI (NetCoMi) so both basins share the same node set (3175 ASVs). Five algorithms (Louvain, Infomap, Fast Greedy, Leading Eigenvector, Walktrap) were run via igraph; partitions were compared with NMI and VI. Iterative consensus clustering (Louvain, 30 runs, tau = 0.5) produced a single partition per basin for interpretation. The pipeline is implemented in R (Rmd) with figures and tables exported for a LaTeX thesis.",
results: "Both networks are sparse (density 0.005, about 25,000 edges each). The Atlantic attains higher modularity than the Pacific for every algorithm; consensus yields 11 communities in the Atlantic (Q = 0.61) and 10 in the Pacific (Q = 0.59). Pacific modules are larger on average and more even in size. The analysis is reproducible from the Rmd pipeline and exports thesis figures and summary tables."

abstract: "This project examines whether blood metabolite profiles of stroke patients differ by time of stroke (day vs night). It uses clinical and metabolomic data from 210 stroke patients from the LMU Hospital Großhadern emergency department to identify metabolites and metabolic pathways with circadian patterns. The goal is to find biomarkers and pathways that associate with time of day of stroke onset.",
methodology: "Raw clinical and metabolomic data are merged and cleaned. Metabolites are classified into KEGG pathways and LIPID MAPS lipid classes via name matching and regex-based rules. After Z-transformation, missing values are handled with multiple imputation (missForest, five runs, averaged). Pathway and lipid-class scores are computed as the mean of standardized metabolite values per pathway or class. Day vs night (07:00–23:00 vs 23:00–07:00) is tested with Wilcoxon rank-sum tests and FDR correction; results are visualized with volcano plots. A sensitivity analysis checks robustness of the lipid classification rules. Lasso regression on pathway and lipid scores, with cross-validation for the penalty, is used for variable selection. The pipeline is implemented in R (Merge script, KEGG download, classification rules) and R Markdown analyses (EDA, classification, pathway scores, imputation, sensitivity, regression, PCA), with outputs written to results and a LaTeX thesis.",
results: "A direct, stable association between individual metabolites and time of day could not be established. On the aggregated level, certain lipid classes and pathways were significantly associated with time of stroke. The lipid class Glycerophosphoethanolamines (GP02) remained significant after FDR correction and was robust across classification-rule sensitivity analyses. Lasso regression highlighted pathways and lipid groups with positive or negative association to day vs night. Results are documented in figures and tables in the repo and in a compiled LaTeX thesis; the full analysis is reproducible via the described R and R Markdown pipeline."
